Abstract. Medical image classification models are ideally expected to identify diagnostically relevant regions while making predictions, yet standard classification losses rarely provide spatial supervision. Explicit supervision via anatomical shape information, such as segmentation masks of task-relevant anatomy, has been shown to guide the network toward regions relevant to the target prediction. However, obtaining such masks incurs substantial manual annotation effort and computational overhead. With the advent of segmentation foundation models that exhibit strong localization of anatomical structures across diverse imaging modalities, we leverage this capability to extract anatomical shape priors without the burden of training a dedicated segmentation model. In this paper, we propose a new framework Locus , an anatomical attention regularization framework that leverages pretrained segmentation foundation models to guide a classifier’s attention toward diagnostically meaningful anatomical structures across diverse imaging modalities . Instead of enforcing pixel-wise alignment with the foundation-model-derived mask, we introduce a regularization term that adaptively balances attention between anatom-ical (foreground) and background regions, penalizing the classifier when background attention dominates. We validate Locus on eight diverse medical imaging datasets spanning dermoscopy, X-ray, histopathology, and cardiac MRI, showing consistent gains in classification performance alongside improved anatomically grounded attention. Our code is avail-able at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/miccai26_LearnFocus-F3EC. 

Keywords: Image Classification · Attention Regularization · Anatomi-cal Shapes. 

## 1 Introduction 

Reliable image classification is central to many high-stakes medical applications, from cancer screening to disease diagnosis, yet medical image classifiers are typ-ically trained with only image-level labels, leaving the network to implicitly determine which regions are diagnostically relevant without any direct spatial arXiv:2607.10851v1 [cs.CV] 12 Jul 2026 2 Hossain et al.

In the absence of such explicit spatial supervision, mod-els learn attention patterns purely from statistical correlations, distributing fo-cus randomly across anatomically irrelevant regions such as surrounding tissue, imaging artifacts, or dataset-specific contextual cues, limiting interpretability and anatomical grounding [18, 19, 37]. Related works. To address this, prior works have explored auxiliary anatomi-cal shape information, for example, encoded as segmentations of relevant struc-tures, to guide classifier attention toward relevant anatomy [5, 10]. To provide the spatial guidance that existing networks lack, a line of work has investigated different ways of directly supervising the classifier’s attention with anatomical structure. First, manually delineated anatomical masks directly constrain the classifier during training [20, 24, 26], but require expert clinicians to annotate the entire training set, making them costly and difficult to scale to large datasets. To reduce this annotation cost, subsequent works instead train a segmentation branch jointly with the classifier and use its predicted masks as the source of anatomical guidance [5, 10]. However, jointly optimizing segmentation and clas-sification is difficult, as the two losses compete for shared gradients and require careful weighting, while noisy segmentation predictions can corrupt the anatom-ical guidance passed to the classifier [5, 10, 23]. Segmentation foundation models (SFM), such as SAM and their medical-domain adaptations [9, 12, 17, 21, 41], have since revolutionized anatomical localization, offering strong, out-of-the-box segmentation across imaging modalities without any dedicated training, making them a reliable and lightweight source of anatomical guidance for the classifier. Another line of research leveraged CAM-based pseudo-masks to constrain fo-cus without requiring pixel-level annotations [14, 30]. However, these approaches face a fundamental trade-off between annotation cost and spatial coverage: aux-iliary segmentation branches achieve near-perfect anatomical coverage but re-Title Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 3

quire expensive pixel-level annotations for training, whereas CAM-based pseudo-masks avoid this annotation cost but tend to activate only the discriminative regions correlated with the target label, rather than the anatomical structures relevant to the prediction [22, 35, 40]. A dedicated segmentation model could instead provide reliable anatomical masks, but training one from scratch rein-troduces the same annotation burden and adds substantial computational over-head [27]. Harnessing the strong anatomical localization capabilities of SFMs, we intro-duce Locus , that for the first time regularizes the classifier’s attention toward class-relevant anatomical structures, entirely removing the costly requirement of dedicated segmentation training. Specifically, Locus introduces a regularization term that adaptively balances attention between anatomical (foreground) and background regions, avoiding rigid pixel-wise alignment with the foundation-model-derived mask and instead penalizing the classifier only when background attention dominates. Our contributions are three-fold: 

– Propose an anatomy-centric attention regularization framework that adap-tively balances classifier attention between foreground and background re-gions using SFM outputs, penalizing the classifier only when background attention dominates. 

– Easily adaptable to a variety of classifier backbones and SFM, serving as a plug-and-play regularization module that requires no modification to the classifier or fine-tuning of the SFM to boost classification performance. 

– Validate on eight diverse medical imaging datasets spanning X-ray, histopathol-ogy, and cardiac MRI, demonstrating consistent gains in classification per-formance and more anatomically grounded, interpretable attention.

We organize this section as (i) developing our anatomy-centric attention regularization term, (ii) describing our patch-wise anatomical prompting strategy for extracting anatomical masks from segmentation founda-tion models, and (iii) designing the overall network loss, supported by the newly designed anatomy-guided regularizer.

2.1 Anatomy-guided Attention Regularization 

Since the state-of-the-art (SOTA) classifier is optimized only with an image-level cross-entropy objective, its attention naturally concentrates on regions that are statistically correlated with the target labels, irrespective of their anatomical validity [3, 6, 39]. However, many classification tasks are inherently anatomy-specific: distinguishing pneumonia from healthy patients depends on the lung regions, grading a skin lesion depends on the lesion boundary, and identifying a bone tumor depends on the affected bone region [13, 25, 28]. Aligning network attention with such diagnostically relevant anatomical structures is therefore Title Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 5

necessary to prevent the classifier from relying on unreliable spurious features, such as imaging artifacts or scanner-specific cues, that fail to generalize across scanners, sites, or patient populations. This motivates obtaining an attention map to compare directly against these diagnostically relevant anatomies. Formally, we compute A from the image encoder E parameterized by ΘE

using Grad-CAM: A = GradCAM (E(x; ΘE )) . To quantify alignment between attention and the anatomical mask M ∈ { 0, 1}H×W obtained from the segmen-tation foundation model, we compute weighted averages over foreground and background regions as

Rather than enforcing pixel-wise alignment, this formulation compares aggregate attention energy within and outside the anatomical region, making the supervision robust to imprecise mask boundaries and spatially varying at-tention distributions. Unlike conventional approaches that apply uniform pixel-wise spatial con-straints, we define the anatomy-centric regularization term as 

R(M, A) = max(0 , Abg − A f g ), (3) which penalizes the classifier only when background attention exceeds foreground attention, rather than enforcing a fixed target ratio between the two. This hinge formulation remains inactive whenever Af g ≥ A bg , avoiding over-suppression of contextual information that may be diagnostically relevant, and only activates a corrective gradient when background attention dominates. In contrast to pixel-wise alignment objectives (e.g., Dice loss), which penalize any deviation from the mask regardless of its diagnostic relevance, R requires only that attention remain anatomy-dominant rather than anatomy-exclusive , and does so without introducing any additional learnable parameters. 

2.2 Patch-wise Anatomical Prompting 

Segmentation foundation models such as Medical SAM3 [12] or BiomedParse [41], when prompted on the full image, often fail to capture all relevant anatomical structures (Fig. 3), either returning a single dominant region or producing empty masks when the target anatomy is small relative to the image canvas. To address this without any fine-tuning or architectural modification to the segmentation model, we propose a simple yet effective patch-wise prompting strategy. Given an input image x, we partition it into a uniform grid of P non-overlapping patches {xp}Pp=1 , where P is selected empirically per dataset based on the ex-pected scale of the target anatomy. Each patch is independently prompted with the same class-derived text descriptor (e.g., bone tumor, lungs, etc.), and the resulting patch-level masks are aggregated via union to form the full-resolution object mask M = SPp=1 S(xp; ΘS ). Union aggregation is deliberately chosen to 6 Hossain et al. 

maximize anatomical coverage, ensuring that all relevant regions contribute to attention supervision regardless of their spatial scale or distribution. Crucially, 

our goal is not to produce precise segmentation, but rather to provide sufficient anatomical grounding to guide classifier attention ; even coarse or partial masks serve this purpose effectively, since the hinge formulation in R penalizes only gross misallocation of attention to the background rather than requiring precise pixel-level correspondence. As illustrated in Fig. 3, when an image contains multiple disjoint instances of the same anatomical structure, such as scattered lesions or bilateral lung fields, prompting a pre-trained segmentation foundation model (e.g., BiomedParse [41] or Medical SAM [12]) on the full image (FIP) often captures the most dominant instance, leaving other anatomically relevant regions unsegmented. Our patch-wise prompting (PAP) strategy addresses this by independently prompting each spatial patch, recovering the missed regions ( ↗) and yielding more complete anatomical masks for guiding the regularizer. Please note that this prompting strategy is not regularized by any learnable parameters, and future work could explore learning the patch configuration end-to-end; our primary motivation here is to introduce a simple yet efficient strategy for obtaining reliable anatomical guidance from a pre-trained SFM. 

2.3 Network Loss 

Now we are ready to define our network loss by combining the classification loss with the anatomy-centric regularizer,

where λ controls the regularization strength. We employ a warm-up strategy where the network is first trained with Ltask alone for Twarmup epochs, then the regularization is gradually increased to λmax over Tramp epochs. Object masks M

are pre-computed offline, avoiding any additional computational overhead during training. While we primarily use Grad-CAM [29] to derive A, our framework is agnostic to the choice of attention extraction method, and other saliency approaches such as GradCAM++ [2], Vanilla CAM [42], or Gradient-Norm can be seamlessly integrated without any modification to R.

## 3 Experimental Evaluation 

Datasets. We evaluate our model across a comprehensive suite of public and private medical imaging datasets covering diverse distinct imaging modalities, anatomical structures, and both static image and dynamic video inputs, pro-viding far stronger evidence of generalizability than single-domain evaluations. The seven public datasets span (i) HAM10000 [31] ( 10015 dermoscopic images, 

7-class skin lesion classification: Actinic Keratoses, Basal cell carcinoma, Benign keratosis, Dermatofibroma, Melanocytic nevi, Melanoma, and Vascular skin); (ii) PneumoniaMNIST [15, 36] ( 5856 pediatric chest X-rays, binary pneu-monia classification against normal); (iii) ChestX-ray8 [34] ( 112 ,120 frontal-view chest X-rays from NIH-ChestXray14, multi-label classification of 14 tho-racic pathologies); (iv) CheXpert [11] ( 1689 chest X-rays, multi-label classifica-tion over 4 observations: edema, atelectasis, pleural effusion and cardiomegaly); (v) BTXRD [38] ( 3746 multi-center musculoskeletal X-rays, binary bone tu-mor classification: benign or malignant); (vi) PanNuke [4] ( 7904 H&E-stained histopathology patches, nuclear type classification across 19 tissue types and 5

cell categories, including neoplastic/inflammatory/connective/dead/epithelial); (vii) BUSI [1] ( 780 breast ultrasound images, two-class breast lesion classifica-tion: benign and malignant). We additionally evaluate on an in-house cardiac cine MRI dataset [33] comprising 510 video sequences, having 24 timeframes per video, for binary classification of myocardial scar presence versus healthy myocardium. Unlike all other datasets, this task operates on temporal image sequences, requiring the model to capture both spatial morphology and cardiac motion dynamics, demonstrating the applicability of our approach to time-series medical data. Please note that we excluded images whose class labels are not primarily determined by anatomical shape or lesion morphology. For example, we exclude the No Finding class in ChestX-ray8 and the normal class in BUSI, as they do not correspond to a specific anatomical abnormality or lesion. 

3.1 Experimental Evaluations Classification Performance. We evaluate the effectiveness of our framework by performing classification across all datasets, reporting accuracy and F1-score. We compare against three baselines, including CE : optimizes the cross-entropy 8 Hossain et al. 

loss solely without any spatial supervision; Masked : considers only anatomical regions using SFM-derived masks, discarding potentially relevant contextual in-formation; and MGA [32]: aligns attention with ROI masks via a Dice-based loss but requires expert annotations and applies a fixed uniform spatial constraint without pathology-specific adaptation. In contrast, our framework requires no manual annotations and adaptively controls anatomical focus. 

Attention Visualization and Analysis. To qualitatively validate that our approach guides models toward anatomically relevant regions, we visualize and compare attention maps of our model against all baselines using GradCAM [29]. 

Effectiveness of Our Regularizer. To isolate the contribution of our proposed regularizer R, we compare classifiers trained with task-specific loss ( Ltask ) (for classification, it is standard cross-entropy term) alone against Ours ( Ltask + R)across both convolutional (ResNet, ConvNeXt) and transformer-based (ViT) network backbones. We visualize GradCAM attention maps on Breast Ultra-sound and Chest X-ray images for each backbone to assess how much anatomical grounding the regularizer recovers relative to Ltask in each setting. We include ConvNeXt and ViT backbones alongside ResNet in our evaluation of network’s attention-quality and robustness analyses to assess generalization across convo-lutional and transformer-based architectures. 

Generalizability of Attention Regularization. To demonstrate that our anatomy-centric regularization is not tied to any specific saliency method, we evaluate its compatibility beyond GradCAM by presenting attention visualiza-tions using GradCAM++ [2], Vanilla CAM [42], and Gradient Norm. For each method, we quantitatively report classification performance and qualitatively compare the resulting attention maps with and without our proposed regulariza-tion, showing that the anatomically coherent behavior induced by our approach generalizes across diverse visualization techniques. 

Ablation and Robustness Analysis. To assess the sensitivity of our frame-work to the regularization weight λ, we ablate its value on the ResNet backbone, reporting accuracy, precision, and F1-score against the CE-only baseline. We further evaluate robustness under distribution shift by testing our model on Chest X-ray images corrupted with varying levels of brightness, contrast, Gaussian blur, and Gaussian noise, applied only at test time, comparing Ours against the CE-only baseline across ResNet, ConvNeXt, and ViT backbones. 

Implementation Details. For classification, we employ standard CNN back-bones with cross-entropy loss Ltask , optimizing network parameters using the Adam optimizer [16]. For segmentation foundation models, we consider Biomed-Parse [41] and Medical SAM3 [12]. However, our framework is agnostic to the spe-cific choice of segmentation foundation model and generalizes to any pretrained text-guided segmentation model.
